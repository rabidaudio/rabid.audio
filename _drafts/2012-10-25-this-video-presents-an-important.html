I was previously unaware that there are ways, using cryptography, to limit information given to exactly what you want to give and nothing else (see Andrew Yao and his Millionaire's problem).
One of the questions Narayanan poses is why companies like Facebook and Google don't implement these. There are some technical and legal answers, but the big one is economic. Those companies have turned their commodity into information. Privacy is then your payment for the service.

That got me thinking about how you would create a business or organization that could implement these systems. The most obvious option is to charge people for such services. However, people don't like paying for things like webmail. Also, money transactions, to a certain extent, make keeping privacy more difficult (although bitcoins have helped on that front a lot).
Then it occurred to me that everyone has a commodity that is of value to the system that could be traded for the service. Everyone has a reasonably high-end computational device and a small amount of internet bandwidth (they must if they are able to use the service). Once I realized this, the answer was quite clear: distribute the serving of the service among it's users. That is what the whole cyberpunk revolution is about.

Let's design systems that distribute serving among it's users. P2P could be used for any type of service; mail, data storage, crypto systems.
